# Seasonal Cash Flow Management

## The Cash Flow Challenge in Events

Event management businesses face significant cash flow volatility due to:
- **Seasonal demand**: Revenue concentrated in peak periods
- **Fixed costs**: Staff and overheads continue in quiet periods
- **Payment timing**: Deposits early, final payments late
- **Vendor commitments**: Often paid before client pays balance

## Pinnacle Events Seasonal Pattern

### Revenue Distribution
| Period | % of Annual Revenue | Typical Events |
|--------|---------------------|----------------|
| Jan-Feb | 8% | Quiet, holiday period |
| Mar-May | 28% | Autumn corporate season |
| Jun-Aug | 12% | Winter slow period |
| Sep-Dec | 52% | Peak season, Christmas |

### Cash Flow Stress Points
- **January-February**: Lowest revenue, costs continue
- **June-July**: Second quiet period
- **August**: Costs rising before September revenue arrives

### Cash Flow Relief Points
- **March-April**: Deposits for spring events arriving
- **September**: Peak deposits plus autumn event finals
- **November**: Highest collection month

## Payment Structure Strategy

### Client Payment Terms
```
Booking:     30% deposit (secures date and starts planning)
Day -30:     40% interim payment (covers vendor commitments)
Day -7:      30% final balance (before event)
```

### Why This Structure Works
- Deposit covers initial planning investment
- Interim payment funds vendor deposits and confirmations
- Final payment before event ensures collection
- Client has payment points aligned with planning milestones

### Exceptions
- Government clients: Often pay on 30-day invoice terms (higher risk)
- Large corporate: May negotiate different terms
- Weddings: Sometimes 50/50 split preferred

## Vendor Payment Management

### Payment Timing
- Venue deposits: Paid when client deposit received
- Major vendors: Deposit on booking, balance before event
- Day-of vendors: Payment within 7 days post-event
- Contractors: Per event, paid within 14 days

### Cash Flow Implications
- Vendor deposits often exceed client deposit received
- Need working capital buffer for gap
- Build vendor relationships that allow payment flexibility

## Cash Flow Forecasting

### Rolling 13-Week Forecast
Updated weekly with:
- Confirmed event revenue and timing
- Probable events (weighted by likelihood)
- Known expenses and payroll
- Variable costs tied to events
- Discretionary spending

### Forecast Categories
**Cash In:**
- Deposits received
- Interim payments
- Final payments
- Miscellaneous income

**Cash Out:**
- Payroll (fixed)
- Rent and overheads (fixed)
- Vendor payments (variable)
- Marketing and admin (variable)

### Warning Thresholds
- Minimum cash buffer: 2 months fixed costs
- Amber warning: Below 6 weeks buffer
- Red warning: Below 4 weeks buffer

## Strategies for Managing Seasonality

### Building Reserves
- Target 3-month cash reserve
- Allocate % of peak revenue to reserves
- Resist spending peak profits immediately
- Reserve for January-February specifically

### Revenue Smoothing
- Pursue government/corporate work (less seasonal)
- Offer off-peak incentives
- Develop winter event packages
- Retainer arrangements with repeat clients

### Cost Management
- Reduce casual hours in quiet periods
- Defer discretionary spending to peak
- Negotiate annual vendor agreements (spread payments)
- Review all subscriptions and recurring costs

### Financing Options
- Line of credit facility for short-term gaps
- Invoice factoring (not preferred, expensive)
- Seasonal business loan (for known patterns)
- Owner capital injection if needed

## Financial Reporting for Seasonality

### Monthly Reports Include
- Revenue vs seasonal budget (not flat monthly)
- Cash position vs forecast
- Accounts receivable aging
- Upcoming payment obligations
- Rolling forecast update

### Board/Management View
- YTD performance vs seasonal budget
- Cash runway projection
- Reserve status
- Risk events and mitigations

## Case Study: The January Gap

**Scenario:**
- December closes with strong revenue ($450K month)
- January projected: $85K revenue
- Fixed costs: $180K/month
- Vendor payments from December events: $120K

**Cash Flow:**
- December closing cash: $320K
- January revenue received: $85K
- January costs paid: $300K
- January closing cash: $105K

**Management Actions:**
- Defer non-essential December spending
- Accelerate November/December collections
- Delay January vendor payments where possible
- Reduce January casual hours
- No owner drawings in January

**Outcome:**
- Managed through gap without external financing
- February deposits start arriving mid-month
- March cash position recovers to target
